Vocabulary Notes

alauda: Just as a bit of trivia, this is the etymological ancestor of the French alouette.

būbō, strīx: The former is more of a general word for owl, but it can be used to distinguish between the different sounds characteristic of different owls, hooting or screeching. Another noun for owl is ulula, -ae, related to ululō, -āre, which can mean shriek or howl. The noun būbō also refers to the primary symptom of bubonic plague.

corvus, passer: Passerine birds (Order Passeriformes) include not just sparrows, but jays, wrens, crows and other corvids, and dozens of other families.

gallus: Small g for poultry, capital G for the Celts who lived northwest of Italy. Then again, the ancient Romans did not use lowercase letters, so chickens and Gauls would be spelled the same.

rōstrum: This can also mean the prow (nose) of a ship, as well as a platform with a pointed lectern for public speaking or dramatic recitations. On mammals such as dogs and horses, it is not the same as the nāsus, but that which connects the nāsus to the rest of the faciēs.

Exercise LXX: Usages of Birds

Thus far we have covered about 30 functions for nouns, 12 of which are (or can be) represented in Latin by the Ablative case. The sentences below have birds or parts or birds playing 18 of those 30 grammatical rolles. Add the missing plural endings for each bird or bird-part, and for any adjectives that modify the birds, based on its use in the sentence.

  1. Diē nātālī meō cōniunx mihi duodecim alaud dedit.
    alaudās
    Accusative direct object
  2. Avēs āl nōn sōlum volant sed etiam sē dēfendunt.
    ālīs
    Ablative of means
  3. Frusta (pieces) pānis anāt amīc prope lacum dedimus.
    anātibus amīcīs
    Dative indirect object
  4. Per agrum cum ānser vagāta sum.
    ānseribus
    Ablative accompaniment
  5. Aquil expuls , parva animālia in silvam rediērunt (returned).
    Aquilīs expulsīs
    Ablative absolute
  6. Cūr lapidēs ad av cōniēcistis?
    avēs
    Accusative place to which
  7. Nōn potuī dormīre propter clāmōrem ist būbōn
    istōrum būbonum
    Genitive object of "of" (could also be a possessor)
  8. Intellēxī columb nōn rectissimē (very straight) volāre.
    columbās
    Accusative subject of infinitive (indirect statement)
  9. Ova gallīn servāmus in calidā camerā.
    gallīnārum
    Genitive possessor (could also be an object of "of")
  10. Quam vēlōcissimē ā falcōn ēsurient fūgimus!
    falcōnibus ēsurientibus
    Ablative place from which
  11. Multum frūmentum ā passer ēsum est.
    passeribus
    Ablative personal agent
  12. Vīsus (The sight) nostrī canis pāvōn nōn placuit.
    pāvōnibus
    Dative object of certain intransitive verbs
  13. In hāc prōvinciā sunt avēs pulcherrim penn.
    pulcherrimīs/-ārum pennīs/-ārum
    Ablative or Genitive of description
  14. Trēs annōs inter psittac Āfricae vīxī.
    psittacōs
    Accusative object of certain prepositions
  15. M. Gracchus gladiōs acūtiōrēs rostr aquilārum facit.
    rostrīs
    Ablative of comparison
  16. In horreō vestrō pauc strīg multōs mūrēs rapiēbant.

Some English Derivatives

Again, beyond the obvious derivatives from the Related Adjectives:

āla, ālātus: alate, axillary (from the diminutive āxilla)

columba: columbine (a flowering plant)

corvus: corvid

penna: penne (quill-shaped pasta)

rōstrum: rostral

strūthiō: struthious, struthiominus
