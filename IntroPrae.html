Latin for the End of the World

The global climate is going bonkers, and we have very little time to act toward preventing destruction on an almost-unimaginable scale.

Civilization is collapsing, along with the unraveling of vital institutions and social norms.

And in the back of your mind, a voice is saying, Y'know, I've always wanted to learn Latin. There isn't much time left, so I'd better get started.

This site began as an attempt to write a Latin textbook for language geeks, home-schoolers, and others to teach themselves the Latin language—or at least a good portion thereof. It is also designed to be somewhat entertaining—or, at least, not as dry as most Latin texts.

We haven't created a fictitious Roman family, like the one found in the Ecce Romani series, to populate these pages. We didn't hire an illustrator to draw typical Romans, or even shell out a few bucks for stock images to decorate the lessons. (We might provide links to pictures here and there.) We don't have Latin dialogs or narrative paragraphs to introduce or conclude each lesson. Instead, we're relying on snarky commentary and semi-useful trivia to keep things lively.

Each lesson starts with some vocabulary, mostly words that have something in common. In some lessons you may also find related words or phrases to give the vocabulary an extra dimension—e.g., when you learn the noun equus (horse), you may also learn the related adjective equīnus (horselike, horse-related), whence comes the English derivative equine.

We are also assiduous in helping you distinguish among words that appear similar but whose meanings are unrelated. For example, the English words equal and equity have nothing to do with equus; they come from the adjective aequus, meaning fair, just, equal. (During the Medieval Era, the ae diphthong in aequus devolved into a simple e.)
