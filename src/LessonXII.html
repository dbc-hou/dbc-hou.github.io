XII. Review I

Congratulations! You have made it to the end of Volume I! You should now know:
  • about 150 additional words, plus some male-female noun pairs like deus/dea
  • the six cases and their basic functions for Latin nouns and adjectives
  • agreement of adjectives and nouns in case, number, and gender
  • how to conjugate -āre and -ēre verbs in the present tense—i.e., making a verb agree with its subject in person and number
  • how to conjugate six irregular verbs in the present tense
  • translation of phrases and simple sentences Latin to English and vice versa.

Exercise XI: Latin to English

Translate the Latin questions and answers below into English. There are quite a few Dative forms, and there may be more than one way to translate them, so choose the translation that works best for you.

  1. Cui histōriam Gnaeī Pompeiī narrās?
    Meō discipulō histōriam narrō
    To whom are you telling the story of Cn. Pompeius?
    I am telling the story to my student.
  2. Quid malō pīrātae dēbētis?
    Pīrātae multum cibum vīnumque dēbēmus.
    What do y'all owe the evil pirate?
    We owe the pirate much food and wine.
  3. Dē (about) quō, domine, est tua fābula?
    Fābula mea dē Mārcō Antōniō est.
    About whom* is your story, master?
    My story is about M. Antonius.
  4. Quandō scrībae nūntiant virīs nōmina (names) aurīgārum?
    Nunc nūntiant nōmina. When do the scribes announce the charioteers' names to the men?
    They are announcing the names now.
  5. Quem mōnstrātis vestrae magistrae?
    Magistrae nostrae poētam mōnstrāmus.
    Whom are y'all pointing out to your teacher?
    We are pointing out the poet to our teacher.
  6. Dāsne puerīs multa dōna?
    Minimē, sed puerīs magnam cēnam semper dō.
    Do you give the boys many gifts?
    No, but I always give the boys a big supper.
  7. Quō nunc ambulat Publius Tullius?
    Ad tabernam laetī agricolae ambulat et frūmentum spectat.
    To where is P. Tullius walking now?
    He's walking to the happy farmer's shop and looking at the grain.
  8. Quō īre vultis, meī fīliī?
    Īmus ad vīllam Aulī Portī, cui** parvam vaccam damus.
    Where do you want to go, my sons?
    We are going to the farmhouse of A. Portius, to whom we are giving a small cow.

* Dē quō can also mean about what.
** In this use of cui, it is a relative pronoun, introducing a relative clause; it still translates as to whom, but not as part of a question.
